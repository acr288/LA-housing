Stories from the City

Los Angeles County is built on the ancestral land of the Gabrielino-Tongva Indian Tribe. This project is dedicated to them, and to all those whose life is connected to this land.


Inglewood

The city of Inglewood is predominantly black and Latino neighborhood in Los Angeles County, home to the Los Angeles Forum, Hollywood Park racetrack, and more recently, the SoFi Stadium. Grappling with segregation and desegregation throughout the history of greater Los Angeles, Inglewood emerged as a Black city, popularized through movies and song, particularly Tupac’s “California Love”. Despite this, the city suffered great financial trouble in the 2000s and 2010s. The Lakers and the Kings left the Forum for the newly built Staples Center (crypo.com arena today), and the venue fell into disrepair. For this reason many were excited when the news of a football stadium broke. The Forum, under new ownership, was also renovated and reopened in 2014. However, the stadium construction also brought fear of gentrification and displacement to the traditionally minority, working-class city. The soaring housing costs in the County also made the relatively affordable Inglewood, with its proximity to the airport and the coast, attractive to white residents. According to this map, Inglewood, as well as surrounding historically Black neighborhoods such as Baldwin Hills and Leimert Park, have undergone some of the greatest housing price increases in the County. Since the opening of the stadium, which hosted its first Superbowl in 2022, housing prices and rents in Inglewood have risen even more. It remains to be seen whether this pattern will be more beneficial or detrimental to its long-time residents.


Silicon Beach

Beach real estate is always pricey, but it has not always been the case on the LA County’s Westside coast. Venice Beach, conceived as a resort town that later became an oil field, was home to a diverse population, the Beats, Hippies, and all sorts of bohemians. Santa Monica, in the 1970s, became a center for renter’s rights movement (leading some to call it “Soviet Monica”). In 2010s Santa Monica and Venice, as well as beach areas to the south, became home to hundreds of tech companies and startups. Venice has experienced some of the highest median home price increase; the coastal Westside is now one of the most expensive areas in the County, with median home prices well over a million.




Boyle Heights/East Los Angeles

Boyle Heights, and nearby predominantly Latino communities have undergone some of the most rapid gentrification in the County, due to proximity to Downtown, making homes and rents unaffordable for many long-time residents. These historic areas possess much hip factor to younger, whiter, wealthier residents, some becoming “hipster” enclaves. Residents of Boyle Heights, once home to Jewish immigrants, and later the jewel and bastion of Latino culture, are fighting back against this wave of gentrification, famously protesting new artisan coffee shops and art galleries. The wounds are fresh, as these areas have been notorious for displacement of minority people, such as the Chinese community razed to build the Union Station, or Chavez ravine neighborhood that disappeared beneath the Dodger Stadium. More recently, freeways have crisscrossed poor and minority communities around Downtown. Boyle Heights is surrounded by freeways on all sides.
